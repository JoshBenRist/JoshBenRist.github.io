The Left Hand of Darkness
Ursula K. Le Guin
June 28, 2026

Left hand of darkness is, I think, pretty firmly in the upper echelons of the sci-fi space. Years ago when I had far more free time and was reading nearly a book a week I really started sinking my teeth into the sci-fi and fantasy genres and worked quite a while at clearing off a ton of the classics or percieved best of the best. Left Hand of Darkness inevitably came up quite a bit.

When I first heard about it years ago I learned very little. I, at some, point got that it was an exploration of a genderless society but I never got more information than that. Because of that blind ignorance this book has easily created the largest divide between my expectations of what a book would be and what the actual book I read was.

It took me about a month to get through, reading off and on during my breaks at work. Now that I've finished the book I keep thinking about that initial description and the idea of this book I had in my head. I can't help but think it reveals a great deal about my internal biases. That initial descprition, an exploration of a genderless society, conjured in my head notions of Tarzan first. From the sci-fi space I imagined the priests tale in Dan Simmons' Hyperion or the Pequinino's in Orson Scott Card's Speaker for the Dead. I even thought of real world examples like Jim Elliot, a missionary who tried to evangalize to a nearly uncontacted tribe in Ecuador and was eventually killed by them.

All these imagined parallels have a common thread. The placing of one party as the intelligent, the civilized. A party that is coming in to graciously bring up this other out of their natural state and up into enlightenment. I realized that I had already framed this exploration of gender and this group created to explore the concept as lesser and the party I imagined exploring that as the dominant right one. It was kinda sad and a little shameful to look internally and realize that is what I had done. In finally reading the book I realized just how much more this book is.

The Left Hand of Darkness in sequence is a story of political intrigue, then a story that closely resembles Endurance by Alfred Lansing, recounting Shackletons escape of Antarctica, and finally a story of deep and touching friendship. From the very first chapter it is clear that the plots focus is not on exploring this genderless society. Instead we are set to following a emissary landed on this planet whose goal it is to convince the governments that be to join a intergalactic collective. through his learning of this world and through him trying to achieve that goal we slowly grow to understand how these lovely little countries work. Through that learning of cultures and customs we also see how said customs and culture differ due to their nature, they are genderless. Its so much subtler and clever than I could ever conjur and again puts my initial ideas to shame. All this to say that Le Guin truly is a master of her art.

In that initial description I drew stick figures in my head where Le Guin painted vast works in oil. We are introduced to our Main Character, Genly Ai, and see the world through his eyes. Those of a career politician/negotiator sent here to calmly explain and convince whomever he needs to. Through him we learn of these two nations that I can only say resemble quaint small little European countries that never were. Their planet is one of near eternal winter so their customs and lifestyles differ from our own and again due to their genderless nature there are also other differences that subtly change the way they interact with eachother. As the book goes on I joined our main character in becoming more and more familiar with these people and their ways. Growing to love them and understand them till perhaps the second most moving scene to me near the end of the book where Genly once again sees his own people. A mixed group, gendered, boys and girls, and feels a sense of wrongness among them.

There exists that upper echelon for a reason. Books that are foundational to a genre both because of their quality and because they do what they set out to so well that it leaves you thinking about it for years to come. Off the top of my head Hyperion, Speaker for the Dead (and honestly the entire Enders Saga), and Asimovs foundation series are all books that I find myself drifting back to and contemplating whether its time to read them again. To experience that story once more. I have no doubt that, given time, The Left Hand of Darkness will join their number.

What a delightful movie. The director of Oldboy directs a adaptation of a Donald Westlake novel. What more could I ask for?

A great movie. I adored the awkward unprofessional nature of our main character and the bizarre premise adapted from the book. Some of the scenes and scenarios conjured were great. I think the first act climax where we get to our first kill was probably the best instance of the comedy and awkwardness at play but it remains throughout.

I'm not sure if it was the fact that I was a bit tipsy while watching, or that I was reading subtitles or just that I've cooked my attention span but I did find this movie to be a tad slow at times. Felt like it coulda been a bit shorter but I think that's probably just me.

While looking through the directors filmography I noticed Decision to Leave which was a oscar darling a few years back and I had completely forgotten about. I also realized that I hadn't finished the vengeance trilogy. Gotta get to those sometime soon while I'm still thinking of them.

Disclosure Day
Stephen Spielberg
2026
June 16, 2026

I actually quite liked this movie. It took a subject I like, The Greys and alien conspiracy mythos, and went in a direction I've not really seen put to the big screen before. Between X-Files and a bunch of deep dives into the Delta Green ttrpg lore I've grown quite fond of that bundle of ideas and it was neat to see this movie use that as a starting point and then extrapolate it out. Now that I've had time to think, the bit I like the most is that it took its titular concept of "Disclosure Day" and played it through.

I'm sure there are easy counter examples, but this is the first instance in my mind, and at this scale, of telling this kind of story. One of revealing to the world the existence of aliens. I feel like all the media I can think of off the top of my head involves like some big reveal at the beginning as your rising action. You know the classic multilingual news reel of aliens attacking New York or London or what have you. That kinda stuff. So seeing that concept of "what if its real and has been for a while and what if we told the world" was cool.

In talking with a friend I brought up that the X-Files is kinda predicated on this general concept. Mulder knows that the supernatural and extraterstrial exist and spends the entire show trying to reveal these shadowy secrets but is always foiled by the grand government conspiracy. Disclosure Day posits "what if Mulder won"? What happens next. I feel like the movie made a bunch of choices to get there that might have people scratching their heads but the exploration of that concept, I think, is what I enjoyed most.

When it comes to public reception it seems opinions are mixed. I went with my sister and I left the theater happy, while she felt muddled about it. Something felt off that she couldn't quite articulate. I later discussed it with a friend who also didn't care for the movie and was able to explain his stance. For him his main points of contention is that the movie:

  1. Didn't feel realistic in how the narrative flowed, whether it was our main characters actions or societies reactions, or the use of force, or lack there of, to capture our main characters and keep the secret.
  2. There were broader themes the film brought up but then did not invest time into to thoroughly flesh out and explore them. I think the primary, and easiest, example is the interplay between the existence of extraterstrial and one's personal faith in the divine.
As we discussed reasonably like rational adults I think I slowly understood what he was sticking on. We ended our discussion with the idea that we both entered into the movie with our own perspectives guiding us and were looking for different things. Generic to say but hang in there. I, as stated above, started the movie and in the opening 20 minutes got told that this is a story about The GreysTM. I love them and this instantly primed me to start thinking about all the stories and media I've seen of them and then I eagerly watched to see what this movie had to say about it. I was then delighted to see a take I hadn't really seen before in conjunction to them.

My friend, on the other hand, came into this movie hot off of a previous Spielberg, West Side Story, which he tells me is full of beautiful themes and explorations of different ideas and what not. So he had that in his head going in and then was dissapointed when the movie didn't really explore anything to the depth of that movie and instead introduced a scattershot of ideas and didn't commit, in his eyes, strongly enough to any of them.

In the end I understand where he's coming from. But I think we both were just looking for different things in the movie. I quite enjoyed it and honestly wouldn't mind seeing it again when it comes to streaming/dvd. This movies not hitting any lists for me but I appreciate what it did and thought it was cool.

Ooo
NamaTakahashi
June 10, 2026

What a delightful little game. Shoutout to Jacob Geller for shouting out Nebula which led me to him doing a video with Razbuten which led me to Razbuten's Nebula content which led me to him and others discussing games from 2025 and eventually discussing this game.

I need to play more games like these I think. Games that are short and well made. I didn't even break 2 hours on Ooo but I still felt incredibly satisfied with it. Its just a super duper tidy game. It has one simple mechanic and iterates on different ways to use it. Each stage teaches you another cute little trick and then eventually your expected to layer your tricks together. That last bit is what got me a bunch. The game is clever. So clever. Everytime I got stuck I felt like an absolute idiot when I eventually got the solution because the solution is always to look back at the tricks you aren't currently thinking of. Usually from a stage or two back.

Also its just cute as hell. Its a very simple game but the aesthetic of your little caterpillar with bombs just works so well. My only criticism might be that the music was a tad repetitive on certain stages but honestly that's not much of a gripe.

Given how short this game was I'm incredibly tempted to return to it again at a later date.

Stop Making Sense
Jonathan Demme
1984
June 10, 2026

This has been in the corner of my mind for ages. It's one of those movies that I've mentally tucked away on a list of things I ought to watch to further my cultural literacy. Now I've finally done it. I've watched it.

My first thoughts are as follows: How in hell does David Byrne have so much energy!? It's amazing to watch. He is constantly moving through out this entire nearly hour and a half set. And almost always in fascinating unexpected ways.

The energy of the entire thing is electric. I love how it starts with just Byrne and a guitar and then as each song comes and goes another member of the band is added to the entourage. Once the whole bands on stage after a few songs they then start playing around with the sets and lighting. Always keeping it interesting. And all the while everyone just has so much energy. It's really something wonderful to watch.

My first introduction to this was seeing a clip of Byrne dancing with the lamp and then later down the line another clip of him with the big suit. I reckon I'll be back to this again. The whole thing is fascinating.

Very atmospheric. I really enjoyed it on in the background as I wrote cause it just sat there and melded with itself, exploring different sounds slowly. It's kinda what I expect from LudoWic. I didn't care for the little digital distortion sound he added that made it sound like my headphones were breaking. That, I think, would actually drive me to insanity very quickly if exposed to it for too long.

007 First Light
IOI Studios
June 6, 2026

Oh man, what a delight. Ask any of my friends and they'll tell you that I don't really play any single player games, let alone any narrative AAA titles like this one. First light somehow did it for me though. I don't think I realized how much I loved and adored the espionage genre till now. I've watched all the Craig Bond movies, all the Mission Impossibles, and a sundry other various TV Shows and movies on the genre. I've listened to every single Reacher novel. Honestly, it should probably come as no suprise that this game did it for me as much as it did and yet I was still surprised by how engaged I was.

The core of it I think is the characters. Greenway, Moneypenny, Cressida and Monroe, and then later on Isola and even M. All the supporting cast are wonderfully written and portrayed by their respective actors. Each one adds personality and depth to the story. I love Bonds relationship between Cressida and Monroe in the first act. I loved Greenways arc of slowly learning to trust and respect Bond. I love Moneypenny as our guy in the chair but still somehow maintaining a bit of her own personality throughout. Most of all though I love this new Bond.

It's been interesting and honestly a little distressing to see the state of the Bond franchise after No Time to Die, Daniel Craig's last movie. The films hovered in limbo for a few years and then eventually the rights got bought out by Amazon where they now sit as Amazon trys to figure out where they go from here. I think everyone is holding their collective breaths as they wait to see what Amazon does with it and if it will be a real stinker or not. I myself had mentally written it off. I think that's probably another of the reasons this game hit me as hard as it did: I was not expecting to enjoy or even get as compelling and well done of a Bond movie as I got from First Light. It was amazing. The narrative really felt like a tightly paced movie.

Obviously I have my quibles with gameplay. Portions felt a little to linear. A bit too much yellow paint here or there. All of the driving set pieces, when you unhitched your brain from the cool action they were presenting, are pretty strict coridors. The open world mission areas that are the portion of the game that is closest to the devs previous victory in Hitman are not nearly as open as its predecessor. Certain paths are locked down till you hit certain story queues and there are little to no branching options to proceed with your mission...

And yet it all works together. Sure its linear and a bit strict. Sure some of the combat segments got me a bit annoyed. Sure its not nearly as open as Hitman. But all these little bits and pieces and touches of polish come together with the story and wonderful acting to make something that as a whole I loved. I can pinpoint bits that bugged me but as a whole I think I well and truly Loved my experience with this game. Heres to it getting a sequel.

June 4, 2026

The intro for Madman's Eye cought me straight outta left field. Not at all what I was expecting from what I had known about DMB previously.

Wanted to dip a bit deeper into their discography and its always interesting, when you like a early album by a group, to check their current stuff and see if your still into their "current" sound. Judging by this album from 2023, 21 years after Busted Stuff, their sound by in large still sounds very similar (and I mean that as a positive).

It takes a bit for it to click for me and for me to calm down and enjoy it. I think that's alright. Not every song needs to be the catchiest track to get tucked away in a playlist. As a whole I quite enjoyed listening through this album.

Busted Stuff
Dave Matthews Band
June 4, 2026

There was a moment in time where I decided to re-up my efforts in collecting physical media. I revamped my modest little AV setup that includes a thrifted amalgam of a 5 disc cd player, tape deck, and a pretty awful turntable. The turn table actively turns me off listening to my vinyls because it sounds so bad so I turned to the cd player. For the longest time while owning it I only had about 5-10 cd's and even then it was about 3 that were actually worth listening to.

Anyway, I decide that must change. I marched to the thrift store and decide I'm going to keep an eye out for anything good. When I'm looking through thrift stores I find its best to keep a open mind and give grace to anything you find to be intriguing. Given my location your wading through a absolute mirk of christian praise and worship and country but there are always diamonds in the rough. I stumbled upon this little fella and saw the band, Dave Matthews Band, and had a spark of recognition. You see I, for ages, listened to a podcast of three brother shooting the shit and if I were to name 2 artists off the top of my head that I mentally associate with The McElroys its Jimmy Buffet and Dave Matthews Band.

So there I am stood in the music section of Mission Thrift, looking down at this copy of Busted Stuff and I realize that I have never actually listened to DMB. In fact I've not a clue what genre they even are. In an attempt to make a decision I listen to the album on the spot. Using spotify and my earbuds I was pretty into what I was hearing. Busted Stuff is a solid first track... and then Grey Street hit me.

I've listened to a bunch more of Dave Matthews now and I think I'm very safe in saying their songs aren't ones that easily pad a playlist. They're a bit slow to get to the point and sometimes the real gems are mere portions of songs. Fine in the background and wonderful when listening to the album as a whole but don't really keep the motion when your shuffling your liked songs on spotify.

Grey Street is the one exception for me. Not only that for some reason I resonate very strongly with it. Much like the previous Lizzy McAlpine albums it really strikes me when I'm in a glum mood. I find myself listening to it on repeat as a comfort song. I don't know why that is or what about it does it. Maybe the smooth sax. Maybe its the jumping simple chord progression. Maybe its the story its telling. Maybe its that incredible chorus.

Very few songs earn the title of "Played On Repeat for Like A Week Straight When I Found It" but Grey Street is one of them. The only other song recently that has even come close to having that kind of instant impact for me is Encore by Red Hot Chili Peppers but that's a conversation for another time.

Older
Lizzy McAlpine
June 4, 2026

Whenever I listen to Five Seconds Flat I feel like I have to follow it up with Older. It's just kind of a natural progression from one into the other and I adore listening to them as a two part experience. I think Five Seconds Flat has more issolated good tracks. Tracks that I could easily throw onto a playlist and jam out to but Older, I think, is the better experience when listened through.

Tracks flow from one into another and theres something about the instrumentals that really gets to me. Come Down Soon is really the start of it for me; that point where on relistens I remember why I love it. The soft vocals melting into the beat and entrance of the slide guitar. It just hits perfectly for me.

All Falls Down is the other one that stands out. If you pointed a gun to my head and asked me what instruments are featured in this track I think I would probably die. I've got a hunch its a wood wind instrument and its quite obviously one of the larger ones like a basoon or something but I've got absolutely no experience at identifying that kinda thing. Either way its superbe and I love it to bits.

Drunk Running, I feel, is the track most like Five Seconds Flat to me. Soft vocals, simple piano backing that slowly builds and fleshes out with more instruments throughtout. It just mellows me out. I'm also only noticing it now but I think its the use of voice memoes and other soundbites that truly makes it reminiscent of Five Seconds Flat as that was kinda a motif throughout it.

Don't really have much to say about You Forced Me To. The song just eats into my brain. Looping and spinning and worming its way in. The simple guitar, the almost circus like piano melody on a old muffled upright. The halting metered out lyrics. It all is just very sticky.

Vortex wraps up everything about the album to me. While the entire album weaves in and out. Trying different things. Fun instrumentation, playing around with different styles, Vortex brings it all back round. Again I'm absolutely abysmal at trying to explain my thoughts (which is by in large why I'm doing this: as exercise and practice). It starts with vocals and a confident but gentle piano and does what it needs to with just that for the first, like, three and a half minutes and then finally builds back in with the instrumentation we've been hearing throughout the entire album. One by one they all join in and it builds and builds. Climaxing the entire album. It just works and really touches me.

I dunno perhaps I'm a simpleton but I think I'm content to be one.
